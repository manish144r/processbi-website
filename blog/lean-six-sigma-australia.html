Lean Six Sigma in Australia: When to Use DMAIC vs DMADV

Most Australian organisations over-apply DMAIC to problems that need DMADV. Here is how to choose — with industry context across government, financial services, utilities, and manufacturing.

ProcessBI·June 2026·10 min read

Lean Six Sigma consulting in Australia covers a wide spectrum — from organisations who use it as a rigorous statistical improvement discipline to those who adopt it as a framework for facilitating workshops and producing swim lane diagrams. Both are valid; they serve different problems. This article is for organisations who need to determine which approach is right for their context, and specifically which of the two primary DMAIC and DMADV methodologies applies.

What is Lean Six Sigma?

Lean Six Sigma is a process improvement methodology that combines two complementary disciplines. Lean focuses on eliminating waste — activities that consume resources without producing value for the customer. The seven wastes (overproduction, waiting, transport, overprocessing, inventory, motion, and defects) provide a structured vocabulary for identifying what to remove from a process.

Six Sigma focuses on reducing variation — the statistical deviation in process outputs that produces inconsistent quality. Six Sigma at its core is a measurement discipline: you cannot reliably improve what you cannot measure, and you cannot claim improvement without comparing outcomes to a quantified baseline.

Combined, Lean Six Sigma gives process improvement consultants in Australia a methodology that identifies both the waste to eliminate and the variation to reduce, and measures the impact of changes made.

DMAIC vs DMADV: The Core Distinction

DMAIC (Define, Measure, Analyse, Improve, Control) is the right tool when you have an existing process that is underperforming relative to a known standard. The process exists; it is producing outputs; those outputs are not meeting requirements. DMAIC investigates why, fixes it, and controls the fix so it sticks.

DMADV (Define, Measure, Analyse, Design, Verify) is the right tool when the process does not yet exist, or when the existing process is so fundamentally broken that improvement is not the answer — replacement is. DMADV designs a new process from scratch against defined customer and stakeholder requirements.

The mistake most Australian organisations make is applying DMAIC to processes that belong in DMADV. The tell is when the Analyse phase of DMAIC reveals that the root cause is not a problem with how the process is executed — it is that the process was never designed correctly in the first place. At that point, incremental improvement will not close the gap; the process needs to be rebuilt.

When DMAIC Applies in Australian Organisations

DMAIC is appropriate when:

  • An existing process is producing a measurable output, and that output does not consistently meet the required standard
  • The root cause is likely to be within the existing process — variation, waste, or a control failure — rather than in the process design itself
  • There is enough historical process data to establish a statistical baseline (at minimum, 30 data points; ideally 100+)
  • The improvement objective can be expressed as a reduction in a measurable metric: cycle time, defect rate, cost per transaction, error rate

Common DMAIC applications in Australian financial services include: mortgage application processing that has excessive cycle time due to undocumented handoffs; regulatory reporting that produces errors because the reconciliation step is manual and inconsistently applied; and claims processing with high rework rates because the documentation requirement is ambiguous at submission.

When DMADV Applies in Australian Organisations

DMADV is appropriate when:

  • The process is new — being stood up for the first time, or in a new entity, or for a new product or service line
  • DMAIC analysis reveals that the current process is structurally flawed — that no amount of improvement to the existing steps will close the gap
  • Customer or regulatory requirements have changed materially such that the existing process was designed for a context that no longer applies
  • The organisation is implementing a new system that will change how the process works, and designing around the new system requires building the process from scratch

A common DMADV application in Australian government is the design of new citizen-facing digital service processes — where there is no legacy process to improve because the channel did not previously exist. Another is the design of data governance processes for a new Microsoft Fabric tenancy, where the data management process needs to be designed alongside the technical architecture rather than mapped from an existing state.

The Five Phases in Practice

DMAIC in Detail

Define: scope the project, identify the customer (internal or external), define the problem statement and improvement target, and produce a SIPOC and project charter. This phase is frequently under-invested — a vague problem statement produces a vague improvement.

Measure: baseline the current process. Collect data on cycle time, defect rate, volume, and variation. Map the current-state process in enough detail to identify measurement points. Validate the measurement system (MSA — Measurement System Analysis) before trusting the data.

Analyse: identify root causes using structured tools: Fishbone (Ishikawa) diagrams, 5 Whys, statistical analysis (correlation, regression, hypothesis testing where data supports it). The goal is to distinguish root causes from symptoms.

Improve: design and implement the process changes that address root causes. Pilot changes where possible before full deployment. Quantify the improvement against the baseline.

Control: establish controls that prevent regression — updated procedure documents, control charts, automated alerts, or process audits. Without a control phase, most improvements revert within six months.

Lean Six Sigma and Process Automation

The relationship between Lean Six Sigma consulting and process automation in Australia is sequential: LSS provides the methodology for understanding and improving the process before automation tools are applied. This sequencing is not a recommendation — it is a requirement for automation success.

A Power Automate flow built on a DMAIC-improved process will automate the correct, waste-reduced steps. A Power Automate flow built on the current state will automate the waste, the rework, and the exceptions — making them faster, not eliminating them. RPA applied to a broken process is an expensive way to make the problem reproducible at scale.

Lean Six Sigma in Regulated Australian Industries

APRA-regulated financial services organisations, Australian government agencies, and utilities have additional incentives for Lean Six Sigma adoption beyond operational efficiency. Regulatory frameworks increasingly expect evidence of process control — not just process existence. APRA CPS 234, ASIC's RG 271 (internal dispute resolution), and the Privacy Act 1988 all require that organisations can demonstrate that their processes are controlled, monitored, and that defects are detected and corrected.

The Control phase of DMAIC, properly implemented, produces exactly this evidence: control charts, defect logs, process audit records, and documented response procedures. For regulated Australian organisations, the LSS Control phase output is simultaneously an operational control and a compliance artefact.

Common Mistakes in Australian LSS Engagements

Skipping the Measure phase. Organisations frequently move from Define directly to Improve because the "obvious" fix is already known. The Measure phase is not bureaucracy — it establishes the baseline without which you cannot demonstrate that the improvement worked. Skipping it is the single most common reason LSS projects claim success that the data does not support.

Using DMAIC without a Green Belt or Black Belt. DMAIC's statistical tools (MSA, hypothesis testing, control charts) require training to apply correctly. Used incorrectly, they produce false confidence in conclusions. LSS consulting engagements in Australia should be led by a practitioner with demonstrated project experience, not just classroom certification.

Not connecting the improvement to a business outcome. A reduction in processing cycle time from 10 days to 4 days is a process metric. The business outcome is the customer experience improvement, the cost reduction, or the compliance risk reduction it enables. LSS project charters should define both, and the project close-out report should demonstrate both.
